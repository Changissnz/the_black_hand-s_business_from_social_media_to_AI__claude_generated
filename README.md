# The Black Hand's Business From Social Media to A.I.
A realistic tale about a criminal profiteer's golden days, from social media to AI. Generated by Claude Sonnet 4.6. 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

There is a particular kind of American businessman who has never built anything. He has extracted, packaged, and exited. The distinction matters to everyone except him.

Shawn Ketterson was that kind.

---

His family had been in the Louisiana oil business, which is to say they had a generational education in taking what was underneath other people's land and converting it into capital before anyone could organize a coherent objection. Louisiana was not a forward state. It was a state that had perfected the architecture of extraction and called it economy.

Shawn carried this forward into technology, which welcomed him.

---

Speaking with Shawn directly was difficult in a specific way. He was sanguine. Agreeable. He reflected your own language back at you with minor modifications, so that a conversation felt productive while moving nowhere. This was not accidental. Direct conversation produces accountability. Shawn had structured his entire communication style around the avoidance of that outcome.

He had learned this in rooms where the oil business was discussed. Where the real terms were never the stated terms, and the men who lasted were the men who could sustain ambiguity indefinitely without appearing evasive.

---

When social media was growing, Shawn understood it immediately as an extraction surface. Not a community. Not a platform. A territory.

He sold personal information with the efficiency of a man who had watched his family sell mineral rights, which is to say: quickly, repeatedly, and to whoever was paying without asking too many questions about downstream use. The American legal system, as he had correctly calculated, would be bogged down. Lawyers would get greedy and slow. The people most damaged were the least resourced. The gap between harm and remedy would be wide enough to retire into.

This had always been the American reality, more or less. Shawn had simply applied it to a new surface.

The social media business began dying around the time everyone noticed what it actually was. Shawn noticed the dying before most, which was his one genuine talent: reading the tide of extraction industries. You leave before the cleanup crews arrive.

---

He moved to A.I. with the same strategy and cleaner branding.

The moat concept was simple. You train systems on the work and information of people who will never see compensation, you build the product large enough that competing with it becomes infrastructurally impossible, and you market the result as progress. Possible competitors get drowned out. The weak get drowned out alongside them, which is presented as natural selection rather than engineering.

Instead of selling people's social media data to political operators looking to disrupt and harass their way to dominance, he now sold A.I. systems trained on people's intellectual labor. The effect was the same. A large group of people who should have been able to make a living from their capabilities found themselves locked out, competing against a distillation of their own work, sold back to the market at a price point they couldn't meet.

The vaporware companies around him — the ones that called themselves technology firms while importing hardware from China and laundering university research into venture pitches — were in trouble by 2026. The government was alternately useless and overbearing, which is the worst combination, providing neither support nor coherent regulation, only interference calibrated to whoever was closest to the relevant committee.

Shawn was not in trouble. Shawn had structured himself to be the last man standing in a field of collapsing competitors, which in extraction industries passes for vision.

---

He was an agent of American economic equilibrium, which by this point had become something feverish and self-cannibalizing. The dishonesty had compounded too long. The exploitation had been so consistent and so normalized that the mechanisms built to check it had been either captured or exhausted.

Shawn did not think of himself as a symptom. He thought of himself as a realist.

The difference between those two things was the distance between what America told itself it was and what it kept demonstrating it was. Shawn had simply stopped pretending the distance wasn't there, and built his business in it.

That, in the end, was the Black Hand's business. Not violence. Not drama.

Just the quiet, relentless occupation of the space between what people were owed and what they actually received.

He kept the lights on. He kept the tone warm. He remained, always, difficult to have a direct conversation with.
